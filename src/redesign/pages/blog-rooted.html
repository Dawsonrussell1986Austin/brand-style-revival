Rooted in Relationships and Rigor: Connecting Executive Functioning & Restorative Practices

ACES PDSI 7 min read

Across schools, educators are increasingly recognizing the impact that executive functioning skills have on learning, relationships, behavior, and student engagement. Many students arrive at school needing support with self-regulation, organization, cognitive flexibility, working memory, and task initiation.

These challenges can directly affect how students access rigorous learning experiences and navigate relationships within the classroom community. At the same time, educators are searching for approaches that strengthen relationships while supporting students in developing the skills needed to thrive academically and socially. This intersection is where executive functioning and restorative practices connect.

At ACES PDSI, ongoing work around restorative practices, executive functioning, and developmentally responsive instruction has led to the creation of resources, professional learning experiences, and a developing workbook focused on helping educators understand how these areas support one another within school settings.

Understanding the Connection

Restorative practices focus on building strong relationships, fostering belonging, and creating supportive learning communities. Executive functioning refers to the brain-based skills that help individuals manage emotions, regulate behavior, solve problems, stay organized, and navigate challenges. When educators intentionally build relational, supportive classroom environments, they are also helping students strengthen executive functioning skills.

Through proactive community-building experiences such as circles, collaborative reflection, and relationship-centered routines, students practice:

  • Self-regulation
  • Flexible thinking
  • Communication
  • Perspective-taking
  • Working memory
  • Problem-solving
  • Emotional regulation

The goal is to spend roughly 80% of the time proactively building relationships and community — so educators are better positioned to respond effectively during the 20% of moments when conflict or frustration arise.

When proactive relational practices become part of everyday classroom experiences, they help create the conditions for both learning and brain development.

Rooted in Relationships: Professional Learning in Action

In response to growing educator interest, ACES PDSI facilitated a session titled Rooted in Relationships: Teaching Executive Function Skills Through Restorative Practices (K–5). The session focused on deepening understanding of the connections between executive functioning and restorative practices while designing learning environments where students can thrive academically, socially, and emotionally. Participants explored:

  • Brain development and executive functioning
  • The relationship between regulation and learning
  • The role of proactive community-building structures
  • How restorative language supports student growth
  • Classroom strategies that simultaneously strengthen relationships and executive functioning skills

The session emphasized that rigorous learning and strong relationships are not competing priorities — instead, they are deeply connected.

Experiencing the Work Through Interactive Learning

Throughout the session, educators engaged in hands-on experiences designed to model how restorative practices and executive functioning skills can be embedded into everyday learning environments.

Check-In Circles. Participants engaged in both sequential and non-sequential circles to reflect on how relationships support growth for students and adults — modeling how structured reflection strengthens community while supporting self-awareness, listening, and emotional regulation.

Friend Fact Quiz. Participants interviewed and introduced a colleague to the larger group. Simple on the surface, the activity required working memory, communication, attention, and relationship-building simultaneously.

Bridging Scenarios. Small groups analyzed common classroom scenarios — students calling out, conflicts between peers, frustration during writing — and explored restorative responses using affective statements and questions. This helped educators shift from viewing behaviors solely as compliance issues toward understanding the underlying skill development students may need.

The Brain Architecture Game. Using the Center on the Developing Child at Harvard University simulation, groups used pipe cleaners, straws, and weights to physically construct a model of brain development over time, drawing cards representing toxic stress, supportive relationships, and environmental factors. Educators experienced how early experiences and stressors can significantly impact development — and how supportive relationships act as protective factors.

"I love being able to apply classroom scenarios with executive functioning and RP practices. Being able to reflect and practice was so helpful. Also, the brain building was insightful."

Supporting All Educators

This work resonates across roles and grade levels. Classroom teachers, paraprofessionals, interventionists, social workers, instructional coaches, and administrators have all engaged in conversations around how restorative practices and executive functioning intersect within school communities.

Moving Forward

As schools continue navigating increasing academic, behavioral, and social-emotional needs, the connection between executive functioning and restorative practices offers a meaningful framework for supporting students holistically. This work reminds educators that relationship-building is not separate from rigorous instruction.

By intentionally designing classrooms rooted in both rigor and relationships, educators can support not only what students learn, but also how they grow.
