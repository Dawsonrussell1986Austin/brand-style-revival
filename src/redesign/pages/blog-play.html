Everyone Loves to Play: Reimagining Learning Through Play, Relationships & Responsive Practice

ACES PDSI 6 min read

Over the past two years, the ACES PDSI Play-Based Learning Consortium has brought together educators and leaders from multiple districts to explore how play-based learning can support academic growth, executive functioning, student engagement, and developmentally responsive instruction in early elementary classrooms.

What began as a shared exploration of why play matters has evolved into deeper systems-level conversations around how districts can sustainably implement play-based practices within classrooms, schedules, curriculum structures, and school culture.

Play is not an "extra" initiative or a separate block disconnected from instruction. Play is an instructional approach that supports how young children learn, regulate, collaborate, communicate, and make meaning of the world around them.

Building a Shared Understanding of Play

During the first year of the consortium, participants focused on developing a common understanding of play-based learning and its role in early childhood and elementary education. Educators explored:

  • The role of play in child development and learning
  • The connection between play and social-emotional development
  • Soft-start structures that support regulation, belonging, and readiness to learn
  • Opportunities for play within literacy, mathematics, science, and social studies
  • The role of inquiry, collaboration, and exploration in rigorous learning experiences

As the work deepened, districts examined how approaches such as provocations, loose parts, and Building Thinking Classrooms in Mathematics could support student thinking, collaboration, and engagement. Participants also reflected on how play-based practices could coexist with district curriculum initiatives, structured literacy practices, and instructional expectations while remaining developmentally responsive and student-centered.

Learning Across Districts

One of the most impactful aspects of the consortium has been the opportunity for districts to learn directly from one another. Throughout the first year, participants visited classrooms and schools at different stages of implementation to observe how play-based learning could look across a variety of settings.

At Thomas Hooker Elementary School, educators observed Kindergarten and Grade 1 classrooms utilizing a dedicated 30-minute play block, implemented in response to students' developmental and social-emotional needs following the COVID-19 pandemic. Even alongside new curriculum demands and evolving state guidance, the district intentionally protected time for student play and interaction.

Participants also visited Snow Elementary School, where educators have embedded play-based practices into instruction for more than a decade. Consortium members observed preschool and Kindergarten students engaged in a grocery store unit where play supported writing, mathematics, communication, collaboration, and problem-solving skills through authentic learning experiences.

As the consortium continued into its second year, returning districts shared examples of implementation successes, barriers, and adjustments. Teams discussed how they introduced play-based learning to families and staff, redesigned schedules to protect time for play, and embedded play within academic instruction rather than treating it as a separate initiative.

Moving From Learning to Systems Change

As the consortium evolved, districts increasingly shifted from isolated classroom practices toward broader systems-level implementation. Teams examined how schedules, instructional structures, classroom environments, and district priorities either supported or limited meaningful play opportunities for students.

Educators from Cromwell Public Schools analyzed PK–2 schedules across grade levels and identified patterns showing that students often became dysregulated after extended periods of whole-group instruction with limited opportunities for movement, collaboration, or play. Sharing these findings with district leadership led to conversations around implementing soft-start structures across PK–2 classrooms.

Meanwhile, educators from Ansonia Public Schools connected the consortium work to district priorities around executive functioning and restorative practices — focusing on how play-based learning can strengthen problem-solving, flexibility, self-regulation, collaboration, persistence, and communication.

Meaningful instructional change is not about adding "one more thing." It is about identifying coherence across initiatives and creating learning environments that reflect how children learn best.

Why the Consortium Model Matters

The Play-Based Learning Consortium continues to demonstrate the power of collaborative, job-embedded professional learning. Rather than engaging in isolated workshops, participants engaged in ongoing cycles of learning, implementation, reflection, observation, and refinement alongside colleagues from other districts.

Most importantly, the work continues to reinforce that play-based learning is not separate from rigorous instruction. When thoughtfully designed, play supports critical thinking, communication, executive functioning, creativity, problem-solving, and strong relationships while helping students feel connected, regulated, and engaged in school.
